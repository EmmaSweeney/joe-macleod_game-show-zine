I Did Not Want to Be on Television

(But I Got Over It)

By Wendy Ward

I like vintage game shows for the fashion and drunk celebrities but I feel like a loser if I am at home during the week and regular game shows are on. My husband Joe loves game shows—really TV in general—so much that he auditioned for Who Wants to be a Millionaire five times (the maximum) and was finally called up to their studio in Connecticut to be a contestant on the show in 2015.

I did not want to be on TV. I do not like to be in the spotlight. I’m a terribly nervous public speaker, and when I even think of saying something in meetings (including Zoom) I get warm and my face gets red. Once I didn’t read out loud the whole semester of a Shakespeare class even though the professor thought everyone took a turn.

I couldn’t say no to being Joe’s plus-one but I was mad—mad at Joe for putting me in a position (on TV) to possibly blow his chances at answering a question correctly (he would only need me if he didn’t know the answer to a question and I’m sure he wouldn’t blame me if I didn’t either, but still).

So I put my nervous energy into what I’d look like on the TV, as if I could control that. It wasn’t as fun as it sounds to pick out three outfits to wear in photos to send the producers because I was pretty sure I needed to buy new jeans and there wasn’t time (I was also mad about that). I put whitening gel in the Invisalign® trays I still wore sporadically only to end up with a white spot on my front tooth the night before. I packed multiple shoes to wear on a show that I might not even be on and that would certainly not show my shoes. I thought of losing weight. I did not need to lose weight.

On our way out of Baltimore heading to Connecticut the night before filming, I said out loud that I needed a drink because of my nerves and Joe pulled the car over to let me buy a roadie at a liquor store. I had supported his desire to be on Millionaire for months because as much as I hate to admit it, I just didn’t think he’d end up a contestant. Maybe because the ones on game shows seemed odd in a bad way, fake, and/or boring and Joe is none of those things?

We ate a late dinner at a diner near the hotel that was near the TV studio—Joe calmly ate a salad (which he has never ordered at a diner in his life) while I had chamomile tea hoping it would help me sleep instead of another beer which I really wanted but was afraid would make my eyes puffy or affect my performance if indeed I ended up on camera. The next morning I woke up the minute room service would deliver coffee, drank as much as it took to go to the bathroom (relieving [HA] a fear I would go on TV without, erm, going), ironed Joe’s faded tiger shirt, and generally freaked out while Joe ate his room service breakfast and reserved his energy.

Once at the studio, the green room was full of blowhard contestants and their plus-ones. They were as weird/dull as I had imagined and no one I wanted to be friends with. We watched episodes of the show while the producer called couple by couple away. I went to the bathroom to put on make-up which I did not need to do because professional hair and make-up artists who did nothing to Joe totally curled and sprayed my hair and applied full make up. (Much later, after watching Joe’s episodes, a friend told me that I should do whatever I did before being on TV because I looked real good up there. Dude.)

As the contestant’s plus-one, I’m seated in the studio audience where I can’t see Joe’s face and he can’t see me. Which is surreal (and I know, a barrier to cheating) because you want to make eyes with your person up there in the disco round of the game show but instead you are watching him like everyone they bused in from who-knows where. They introduced me after he won a few questions when it was worth getting to know him better, I guess. I waved to the camera in an awkward way I never waved before or since but Joe had called me his Wonderful Wife Wendy so I don’t care.

Joe was a funny, smart contestant but he had this frustrating habit of basically answering the question correctly out loud and then dancing around the other (wrong) answers. They coached us in the green room to verbalize the thinking behind your answer—just yelling “C. my final answer” isn’t super fun TV—but Joe talked about wanting to drink a Guinness (one of the options in one question) after he’d already said Pedialyte (the right answer) but hadn’t locked it in. Joe, me too, but focus, babe!

I’m sitting there amazed at how well he is doing and nervous he’s going to call me up (even though I knew the Pedialyte answer for sure), so I put my weight on one foot and sorta leaned forward because I hoped that would give me the momentum to enter the ring with energy even if I wanted to stay seated like I did that one time on a ski lift, and they had to stop it to help me off.

It ended up not that bad once he called me up to help him answer a question about movie backgrounds: I gave him my thoughts calmly and slowly and he picked the correct answer. I felt like my hair looked amazing and I liked my outfit. Joe grabbed me tight and it was cute. The white spot on my tooth didn’t show. Joe quit while he was ahead (!) and when we went below the stage to the tiny business office to sign tax papers directly after, the accountant said everyone backstage was rooting for us. I mean, geez.

We had to keep our cool once out of the studio so the contestants in front of us didn’t know how we did—we totally saw the couple who went on before looking pissed at each other while walking out. You could tell which one of them fucked up. Waiting to get to the car to scream—and scream we did!—wasn’t that hard because we were in shock.

Months later, the aired episodes didn’t show the studio audience chanting Joe’s name when he was really hot towards the end. I think I remember chanting too—and I swear that, even more than the prize money, made it all worth it.
