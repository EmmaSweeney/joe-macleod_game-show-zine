You Don’t Have to Know Shit About Lena Dunham

A talk with Who Wants to Be a Millionaire Contestant Jim Meyer of Baltimore, MD

When I was trying to get on Who Wants To Be A Millionaire I told everybody within earshot that they should try out. My pal Jim Meyer, a writer and standup comedian, is the only person I know who auditioned. We had different experiences inside the television.

Jim: You were telling me I should audition for months before they even announced they were coming to Baltimore. When you were going up to New York, you were saying, “You should audition, you would be a natural.” And then they came to Baltimore and I just started working at my current job, which is two piers over from where they were doing the interviews, the auditions, so I was like, “Screw it.” It’s kismet. And yeah, so you convinced me that I would be a good fit.

Joe: Yeah, I was trying to get everybody in on it just because it’s like, why not? Why not take a shot at it?

Jim: But you really thought I was kind of a special case.

Joe: Absolutely! So what was your interview experience like, or your tryout? I mean, obviously you passed the test easily.

Jim: It was at this hotel, this kind of fancy hotel in Baltimore Harbor and I was there towards the end of the day. I took the test and they said, I can’t remember exactly what happened, but it’s take the test and then they do an interview. I was sitting there listening, because you’re in that big room, listening to other people be interviewed, and I’m hearing the questions, and I’m coming up with answers to these fascinating questions they had. But by the time I got there, I was literally [the producer’s] last interview of the day.

Joe: Oh, you were listening to the questions about, “Why are you interesting? Do you have any hobbies?” That kind of shit?

Jim: Yeah, and I’m coming up with answers. I get up there, and she’s barely even looking up at me, and all she’s been talking about, as I walked up, is pizza, and she clearly just wants to find a place to get pizza. And I was like, “If you’re looking for a place to get pizza...” And I told her about [local pizza place] Joe Squared. Whether she was interested or not, I have no idea, but she was not focused on me. And then she said, “What would you do with a million dollars?” And I was like, “Fuck, what would I do with a million dollars?” And I immediately began lying and I was like, “I would write a rock opera about my experience on Who Wants to Be a Millionaire and then I would fund the Baltimore Rock Opera Society to put on Who Wants to Be a Millionaire, the musical.” And she’s like, “You’re in.”

Joe: It’s still a solid idea and you should still do it.

Jim: Yeah. I don’t know how to write music, though. Maybe I’ll write a play.

Joe: All you gotta do is write what they call the book, and then you find somebody to write the music.

Jim: So, I don’t know if you remember, you’re sitting in the little—there was like a little hallway off of an emergency exit, that you had to wait in for the makeshift little studio where they had this remarkably hot woman with a camera who would question you.

Joe: It seemed like a closet to me. I don’t know if they’d moved it when I got there, but it just seemed like a tiny room.

Jim: Yeah, it was like a closet, and you were waiting in this little off-hallway with the bathrooms on it. I started chatting with the people around me, everybody was so nervous, nobody was talking. This guy came out, and he and I had been chatting and I was like, “What did they ask you?” I asked him that right before they came out and said, “Don’t talk to anybody as you leave,” but we didn’t know that then, and so he told me the question, which was, “What’s the biggest number, the number of syllables in a haiku, countries in South America, cups in a gallon, or colleges in the Ivy league. And so he told us the question and then we talked it out.

So, I go in and I’m on video and they asked me the same question that I had just heard. All signs point to Jim here, you know? So I’m like, “Well, there, there are 17 syllables in a haiku, 16 cups in a gallon... I don’t know how many colleges in the Ivy league, but it’s hard to get in, so I’m going to guess eight. And then whatever the other thing was, so the answer is a haiku, final answer.” And she’s just stunned that I like rattled it off that fast, right?

Joe: He’s a genius! You’re making yourself to be an appealing contestant.

Jim: Yeah. And then they asked me to tell a story and I went blue, but it killed her because I’m sure no one else that day probably went blue. I told a story about my two sons. I told the story that I used to play this game with Jonah around that time where I would take my wedding ring and he would play a game in the bathtub. I would drop in my wedding ring and he’d have his little toy scuba diver find my wedding ring and then put it in the little toy treasure chest. I had a weird feeling that I shouldn’t give him the ring, but I gave it to him anyway. He looked at it and just put his dick in it. I said, “Yeah, fits right in there, doesn’t it, buddy?” And I decided then and there that on my death bed, I would call both my sons close, and I would give my ring to Leo, the other kid, and as soon as he put it on, I’d be like, “Your brother used to put his dick in that,” and then I’d die. So, I told that story.

Joe: So that destroyed.

Jim: And that’s when they asked us, “So, like, what do you do?” And I mentioned the standup comedy. She clearly didn’t want to hear about that.

Joe: Really?

Jim: Oh, yeah, didn’t want a stand up comic.

Joe: They want a real person. They don’t want pro, I get it.

Jim: So, I just started this job writing for the state and I could tell she was losing interest. And one of the things, when I took the job, I had talked about writing a dad blog for the state because I worked for the Maryland Office Of Tourism and it would’ve been like a tourism dad blog, for where to go with your kids in Maryland. And she’s like, “You’re a dad blogger?” And I’m like, “No, but I’m thinking about starting one, possibly for work.” She’s like, “It’s so cool that you’re a dad blogger. Do you have any good advice?” I’m like, “Whatever.”

So then maybe a week goes by, maybe two weeks at the most. And they call me and they’re like, “You’re on the show.” And I’m like, “That’s great.” And they started rehearsing the stories. They’re like, “They want to figure out what your story’s going to be when you’re on there or how they’re going to pitch it.” You know? And they asked me about my dad blog and I was like, “Well, I don’t actually write a dad blog. I’ve just been thinking about writing a dad blog,” And they’re like, “Well, this dad blog, it’s so fascinating.”

Joe: I was completely dialed in to whether or not they were interested in me, or paying attention to me, and I could feel them lose interest, I could just feel the lack of enthusiasm, you know?

Jim: And maybe that’s a skill they learn to bring it out of you, you know? Maybe they’re just so shut down. Who knows? But I amped it up. So like then you get assigned a producer that calls you again and further works on your story. And again, it’s dad blog, dad blog, dad blog. Yeah. It was the craziest thing.

Joe: So, you had to find somebody to be your plus one.

Jim: My friend Craig Casey was my plus one. He’s just got a brain the size of Belgium. He knows everything. He would have been a great contestant himself. He and I drove up and we had a hotel up there. I don’t know if you remember, it’s an old school that they shoot in. Connecticut, their film industry, the film office there, decided that they were going to corner game shows. Connecticut had tax breaks for game shows like nobody’s business.

Anyway, it’s an old school and you sit in what was probably the teacher’s lounge of the school with all the other contestants watching old episodes of the show with like some turkey wraps brought to you occasionally. And then I was trying to make conversation with these people. I was there for like seven hours in this room. They take all your reading materials, they take your phone, you can’t have a laptop or anything. You’re just there. Periodically, they would break me out and talk about my stories and I’d try and guide them to other stories. But they’re like, Let’s talk more about your dad blog. That’s more of a second story, your second story, that’s going to be good, but first we’re going to do the dad blog.” I’m like, “I’m not a dad blogger!”

We did the rehearsal where they take you down, and I was the first one to do the rehearsal and they let me go on the actual stage so you’re not spooked out by all the lasers when you get there and everything. And I made it to 50K without using a lifeline in rehearsal, and just fucking crushed it. And there was a weird competitiveness. All these contestants are sitting around in the waiting room, just watching old episodes of Millionaire, trying to shout the answer out first.

Joe: Yeah, that annoyed me. I was just like, “We’re not competing, it’s us against the machine. Come on.”

Jim: But I felt so good, and they were definitely building me up. Like, “We think this would be a great commercial for this show, this story, this thing.” They’re about to send me home to come back the next day, and then they had a change of something or other and they’re like, “You’re going,” which sucks because if I’d gone the next day, I’d have had wholly different questions and who knows what would have happened?

They asked me about my dad blog and I was like, “Well, I don’t actually write a dad blog. I’ve just been thinking about writing a dad blog,” And they’re like, “Well, this dad blog, it’s so fascinating.”

They call your name, and you go to your mark. It’s like being fired out of a cannon through a disco ball, into the host of TV’s The Bachelor, like, lasers and crowd, and I was killing it, man. I was dancing as I was answering my questions. All this, If I’d done well, would have stayed in the show, but all my dancing and stuff got edited out because I was such a non-entity as far as how far I made it.

Joe: So how far did you make it?

Jim: Four correct questions.

Joe: Were you being an entertainer or were you nervous?

Jim: I was nervous. I was being entertaining, but I was nervous. I was dancing around and I was cracking wise, and I was doing well. And then the question came, the fifth question, and it definitely was one of those things if I had taken my time, I could have figured it out. “Though it was inked at a strip mall and not the Plaza Hotel, Lena Dunham’s first tattoo was of what whimsical children’s character?” And the answers were A, Madeline; B, Curious George; C, Eloise; and D, Dora the Explorer. I did everything wrong here. I didn’t know the answer. This was a good one to ask the crowd. This is the one that the crowd could have known. Right?

Joe: Right, and there is a clue in the question.

Jim: Yeah. It’s not about—it’s just about this children’s book character. You don’t have to know shit about Lena Dunham.

Joe: No, yeah. Yeah.

Jim: What children’s book character lived in the Plaza Hotel? I started talking it through, which was dumb because when I went to poll the audience, I had colored their opinions.

Joe: Oh, wow.

Jim: So I said, “Well, I know it’s not Curious George. And I know it’s not Dora the Explorer,” so I already screwed that up. So now, half the audience that maybe didn’t know the answer is eliminating those two as well. I’m like, “I know Madeline had some sort of weird upbringing, so maybe that was it, but I don’t know...” So, I polled the audience. And it was 41 for Madeline, 42 for Eloise, so close. I was like, “Fuck.”

Joe: Right.

Jim: Now, I used to work in a children’s bookstore, and if I really thought about it, I could have pictured Madeline, which has the Eiffel tower on the first book. And I would have known France, not the Plaza hotel, but I didn’t take my time. I just blurted out an answer. Because I didn’t want—my thinking was, “I could call up Craig, my plus one, but I had a gut feeling, and I’m like, “If I’m going to get far in this game, I’m going to need at least one lifeline more. I can’t use all of them this early in the game.”

Joe: So you’re looking past the question because you’re reasonably certain.

Jim: I had a hunch and I hurried. And, if I’d taken my time and really thought about it, I probably would have remembered that book cover and gotten it right because to this day, I can picture the Madeline book cover with the water colors and the Eiffel Tower.

Joe: Yeah, Eiffel Tower, yeah.

Jim: So I looked at Chris, the host, and I said “I should know this. I’m a dad blogger.”
